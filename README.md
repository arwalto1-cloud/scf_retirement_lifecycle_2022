

# Retirement Participation and Wealth Accumulation Across the Life Course  
### Evidence from the 2022 Survey of Consumer Finances

## Overview
This project analyzes how retirement participation and typical retirement balances (IRA and employer-sponsored defined contribution accounts) vary by age group using data from the 2022 Survey of Consumer Finances (SCF). The analysis is situated in a post-COVID and inflationary economic context and evaluates whether observed retirement patterns align with life-cycle savings expectations.

## Research Questions

**Primary Question**

How do retirement participation and typical retirement balances (IRA and employer-sponsored accounts) vary by age group in the 2022 Survey of Consumer Finances?

**Supporting Questions**

- How does retirement participation change across the life course?
- How do median retirement balances compare to median income across age groups?
- How do median retirement balances compare to median liquid savings across age groups?
- Do observed patterns suggest accumulation during working years and decumulation after retirement age?

## Data
The analysis uses the 2022 Survey of Consumer Finances (Federal Reserve Board), a nationally representative survey of U.S. household finances. Key variables include age, IRA balances, employer-sponsored retirement balances, liquid savings, and household income.

## Key Findings
- Retirement participation rises through early and mid-career stages, peaking in the 45–54 age group, and declines after traditional retirement ages.
- Median total retirement balances increase through 55–64 and decline thereafter, consistent with decumulation in retirement.
- Median IRA balances are $0 across age groups, indicating that IRA wealth is concentrated among a subset of households.
- Median liquid savings increase gradually with age but remain substantially smaller than retirement balances across the life course.

## Methods
- Cleaned SCF negative missing-value codes prior to constructing aggregate measures.
- Constructed total IRA and employer retirement balances from component variables.
- Constructed liquid savings from transaction account balances.
- Used age groupings to compare participation and median balances.
- Emphasized medians rather than means due to the highly skewed distribution of wealth.

## Limitations
- Survey weights were not applied; findings should be interpreted as descriptive and exploratory rather than nationally weighted estimates.

## Next Steps
- Incorporate SCF survey weights to produce nationally representative estimates.
- Examine variation by income quartile and educational attainment.
- Analyze retirement-to-income ratios to assess adequacy.
- Explore post-retirement asset reallocation patterns.
